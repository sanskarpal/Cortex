The everyday mess

Your Downloads folder is where files go to disappear.

Screenshots, invoices, tax forms, holiday photos, that PDF you swear you saved — all dumped into one giant pile, sorted by nothing but the date you happened to grab them. Finding anything later means scrolling, squinting at thumbnails, and opening the wrong file three times.

You could organize it by hand. But it's tedious, you'd have to keep doing it forever, and halfway through you give up. The usual "smart" cleanup tools fix this by uploading everything you own to a company's servers — trading your mess for your privacy. Cortex was built to refuse that trade.

Under the hood, in plain English

Four calm steps, every time.

There's no magic and no guesswork dressed up as confidence. Cortex moves through the same four stages a careful human assistant would.

STEP 1
👀

It looks

Cortex walks through your folder and notes the basics of each file — its name, type, and size — without opening or changing anything. Think of it as taking inventory.

STEP 2
🧠

It understands

For documents it reads a little of the text; for pictures it actually looks at the image. Two small AI models — one for words, one for pictures — turn “what's in this file” into a meaning the computer can compare.

STEP 3
⚖️

It decides — carefully

It matches each file to the best-fitting category and scores how sure it is. Confident? It's queued to move. Unsure? It's set aside for you. It would rather ask than guess wrong.

STEP 4
📦

It tidies — reversibly

It shows you the full plan first. Only when you say go does it move anything — into the trash-safe, fully undoable kind of move. One command puts everything back.

Built so it can't lose your stuff

Cautious by design, not by promise.

The scariest thing about a tool that moves files is a tool that moves the wrong files. Cortex is engineered so that mistakes are visible, reversible, and rare.

📝

Always shows the plan first

Before a single file moves, you see a complete preview: this file goes here, that one there. Nothing happens until you approve it. The default is to do nothing.

↩️

Every move is undoable

Files aren't deleted or overwritten — they're moved the recoverable way, and every action is written to a log. Changed your mind? One “undo” command rewinds it, and it checks each file is untouched before reversing it.

🙋

“I'm not sure” is a valid answer

When Cortex isn't confident, it doesn't guess — it leaves the file exactly where it was and flags it for you. In testing across 75 files, it made zero wrong moves.

The whole point

Your files never leave your computer.

Most “AI” tools work by sending your data to a server somewhere. Cortex does the opposite: the AI models live on your machine. Your tax documents, your private photos, your work files — none of them are uploaded, copied to the cloud, or seen by anyone but you. There's no account to make, no internet connection required once it's set up, and nothing tracking what you do.

The only time it touches the internet is the very first run, when it downloads the AI models themselves — and even that only happens after you explicitly say yes. After that, you could unplug your network entirely and it would work exactly the same.

Good questions

Things people ask.

Will it mess up my files?

It's built specifically to avoid that. It shows you exactly what it plans to do before doing anything, only moves files in a recoverable way, keeps a log of every action, and can undo everything with one command. When it isn't confident about a file, it leaves it untouched rather than risk a wrong move.

Do I need to be technical to use it?

Right now it runs as a command you type, so a little comfort with a terminal helps — it's currently best for tech-comfortable users. The thinking and the safety, though, are designed for everyone: plain previews, plain categories, and an easy undo.

Is it really private? How do I know?

The entire project is open — anyone can read exactly what it does. It makes no internet connections during normal use, has no accounts or tracking, and the AI runs on your own machine. The one exception is a one-time model download on first setup, which only happens with your explicit permission.

What kinds of files can it sort?

Documents and text (including the text inside PDFs, even scanned ones), source code, and photos and images — invoices, receipts, screenshots, personal photos, books, and more. You can edit the list of categories to match how you think about your own files.

What does it cost?

It's free and open-source under the MIT license. The AI models it uses are also free and open. There's no subscription and nothing to buy.
